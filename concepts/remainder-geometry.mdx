After the base-system filter eliminates ~73% of numbers, the deep neural network must resolve the remaining "intersections" — numbers that look structurally identical under the primorial base rules. The key breakthrough was discovering that the **internal structure of remainders**, when viewed through additional base systems, reveals fractal patterns that sharply concentrate prime probability.

## The prime feedback effect

The first discovery is that the **primality of a remainder is itself a probabilistic signal** for the primality of the original number.

If `N mod M` is a prime number, N itself is statistically more likely to be prime. The reason is geometric: a prime remainder means N has "avoided" the composite footprints of the base-M system. Composites tend to cluster around remainder values that are themselves composite.

| Modulo | Probability shift when remainder is prime |
|:-------|:------------------------------------------|
| Modulo 7 | **+3.93%** |
| Modulo 9 | **+2.95%** |
| Modulo 13 | **+1.95%** |
| Modulo 31 | **+0.98%** |

These shifts are measured against the baseline prime density across the first 10,000 integers (~12.27%). While no individual signal is decisive, the pattern is consistent and monotonically decreasing with the modulo size — larger modulos produce smaller but still measurable signals.

The `neural_encoder.py` encodes this as two binary features:

```python
rem_9  = n % 9
rem_30 = n % 30

dimensions['is_rem_9_prime']  = int(isprime(rem_9))
dimensions['is_rem_30_prime'] = int(isprime(rem_30))
```

## System geometry: remainders in other bases

The second — and more powerful — discovery came from converting remainders themselves into different number bases and examining their digit structure.

The question: does the way a remainder "looks" in Base-3 or in binary tell us anything about whether the original number is prime?

The answer is yes, dramatically so.

<Tip>
**Fractal primality:** Primes don't just exist in Base-10 — they leave a footprint across every numbering system. By cross-referencing how a remainder looks in a second base, you can identify "prime sanctuaries" where the probability of finding a prime is nearly 3× the baseline.
</Tip>

## Clarity clusters

By analyzing `N mod 30` and `N mod 9` across the first 10,000 integers, the following high-density clusters were identified:

| Feature | Condition | Prime density | Lift vs baseline |
|:--------|:----------|:-------------|:-----------------|
| Sum of (N mod 30) in Base-3 | = 5 | **30.83%** | **+150.8%** |
| Sum of (N mod 30) in Base-3 | = 3 | **28.77%** | **+134.1%** |
| Parity of (N mod 30) | ODD | **24.56%** | **+99.8%** |
| Binary popcount of (N mod 30) | = 4 | **23.12%** | **+88.1%** |

Baseline prime density (numbers 1–10,000): ~12.27%.

The most powerful signal — a Base-3 digit sum of 5 for the mod-30 remainder — nearly triples the probability of primality. This is not a weak statistical trend; it is a structural pattern.

### Understanding the top signal

The feature `rem_30_base3_sum` computes:
1. Take `N mod 30` — the last "digit" in Base-30
2. Write that remainder in Base-3
3. Sum the Base-3 digits

For example, if N mod 30 = 17:
- 17 in Base-3 = `122` (1×9 + 2×3 + 2×1)
- Base-3 digit sum = 1+2+2 = **5**

N = 17 is prime, and it lands in the highest-density cluster.

This is encoded directly in `neural_encoder.py`:

```python
rem_30 = n % 30
dimensions['rem_30_base3_sum'] = sum_of_digits(rem_30, 3)
```

## Silent zones

Equally important are the "silent zones" — remainder structures where primality is near-zero:

| Condition | Prime density |
|:----------|:--------------|
| N mod 30 is **EVEN** | **0.02%** |
| Binary popcount of (N mod 9) = 0 | **0%** |

An even value for `N mod 30` means N itself is even — composite by definition (except N=2). The 0.02% residual consists almost entirely of the number 2 itself.

A binary popcount of 0 for `N mod 9` means `N mod 9 == 0`, i.e., N is divisible by 9 — also composite. The 0% density here is exact: no prime is divisible by 9.

These two conditions are encoded as:

```python
rem_9 = n % 9
dimensions['rem_9_bin_pop'] = bin(rem_9).count('1')  # 0 means rem_9 == 0
```

## The fractal interpretation

The pattern across all four remainder geometry features reveals something deeper than statistical correlation. Primes are not randomly distributed across the integers — they occupy specific structural positions in every numbering system simultaneously.

When you look at `N mod 30` in Base-3, you are essentially applying **two layers of base transformation** to N: first reducing it modulo 30 (a primorial), then examining the digit structure of that remainder in a different base. Each layer filters the structural information differently, and the intersection of those filters lands on regions of dramatically concentrated prime probability.

This is the fractal nature of primality: the same clustering that appears in Base-10 (only certain last digits) reappears at a finer scale when you examine the remainders themselves in other bases.

The four remainder geometry features in `extract_all_dimensions()` — `is_rem_9_prime`, `is_rem_30_prime`, `rem_30_base3_sum`, and `rem_9_bin_pop` — give the deep neural network direct access to this fractal structure, allowing it to resolve intersections that pure modular arithmetic cannot separate.
